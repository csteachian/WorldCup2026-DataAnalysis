Step 1 — How to read the data

Before you pick your own squad, let's see how a data analyst thinks. Here are 3 fictional goalkeepers. Only 1 GK can be picked — which one would you choose?

Your stats explained:
🧤 Caps — how many times they've played for their country (more = more experienced)
⚽ Goals — international goals scored (most important for midfielders and forwards)
💨 Pace — speed rating out of 100 (very important for wingers and forwards)
⭐ Rating — overall quality out of 100, calculated from ALL their abilities combined
Three goalkeepers — pick the best one
PlayerPosAgeCapsGoalsPaceRating
Sam TorresGK347205281
Alex MüllerGK274806188
Jo ParkGK19305871
Best pick: Alex Müller (rating 88) — highest rating, good pace (61), age 27 is the prime for a keeper, and 48 caps shows they're trusted and experienced at international level.
Sam Torres? High caps (72) = lots of experience, but age 34 is a risk — older players lose pace and reflexes. Could be a good backup though.
Jo Park? Only 19 and 3 caps — far too inexperienced for a World Cup, even if they have potential for the future.
Key idea: A data analyst never looks at just one number. You need to weigh up ALL the stats together before making a decision. Age + caps + rating together tell a much better story than any one stat alone.

Step 5 — Questions about your squad

Answer each question using the data you used to pick your team. Use the sentence starters to help you.

1. Which stat did you find most useful when picking your squad, and why?
💬 "I found ________ most useful because it helped me to decide..."
Think about the stats in the table: goals, pace, caps, age, rating. Which one did you look at the most? For example — did you sort by rating first? Did you check pace for your forwards? Did caps help you judge experience?
2. Look at your goalkeeper. What data told you they were the right choice over the others?
💬 "I chose ________ as my goalkeeper because the data showed that..."
Compare your goalkeeper's stats to the other GKs you didn't pick. Which numbers were higher? For a goalkeeper, rating and caps are usually more important than pace or goals.
3. Which player in your XI has the highest overall rating? Why do you think "rating" uses lots of stats instead of just one number like goals?
💬 "My highest rated player is ________. I think rating uses multiple stats because..."
A goalkeeper who scores 0 goals isn't a bad player — goals just isn't the right stat for them. If rating only used goals, all the best-rated players would be strikers! Rating combines many different skills so it can fairly compare players in different positions.
4. Did you have to leave out a player you wanted because of the position limits? How did the data help you make that difficult choice?
💬 "I had a difficult choice between ________ and ________. I used the data to decide by..."
If you had 5 great midfielders but only 3 spots, how did you decide who to drop? Which stats mattered most for that position? Did you compare two players side by side?
Extension
5. If you could add one more column of data to the table, what would it be and why?
💬 "I would add ________ because it would help me to..."
Think about what's missing. Could you tell if a player was injured? Could you tell if they'd been playing badly recently? Could you tell how tall a centre back is (important for headers!)? What extra data would make your decision better?
